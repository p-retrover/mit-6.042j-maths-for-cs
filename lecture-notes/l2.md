## 1. Proof by Contradiction (Indirect Proof)
In a direct proof, you start with axioms and previously known theorems, applying logical deductions step-by-step until you reach the conclusion. An indirect proof, specifically a proof by contradiction, approaches the problem backward. 

**Methodology:**
To prove a proposition $P$ is true, you assume the opposite, namely that $\neg P$ (not $P$) is true. You then use this assumption to take logical steps forward until you derive a falsehood (a contradiction where something is proven to be both true and false). Since deriving a falsehood from $\neg P$ means the implication $\neg P \implies \text{False}$ is true, the only way for the implication to hold mathematically is if $\neg P$ is false, meaning $P$ must be true.

### Example: $\sqrt{2}$ is Irrational
This proof was historically discovered by the Pythagoreans, causing a philosophical crisis because it contradicted their axiom that all lengths were finite and thus rational.

*   **Proof by contradiction:**
    1.  Assume for the purpose of contradiction that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational. 
    2.  This means $\sqrt{2}$ can be written as a fraction in lowest terms: $\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers with no common factors.
    3.  Squaring both sides gives $2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2}$, which implies $2b^2 = a^2$.
    4.  Since $a^2$ is a multiple of 2, $a^2$ must be even. This implies that $a$ itself must be even. 
    5.  Since $a$ is even, it can be written as $a = 2c$ for some integer $c$. 
    6.  Substituting this back into the equation: $2b^2 = (2c)^2 = 4c^2$. 
    7.  Dividing by 2 yields $b^2 = 2c^2$.
    8.  This implies $b^2$ is even, and therefore $b$ must also be even.
    9.  **Contradiction:** We have established that both $a$ and $b$ are even. This means they share a common factor of 2, contradicting our initial assumption that $\frac{a}{b}$ was a fraction in lowest terms. Therefore, $\sqrt{2}$ must be irrational.

## 2. The Danger of False Proofs
To emphasize the need for rigorous, analytical steps, the lecture demonstrates a "Proof by PowerPoint" where it is visually "proved" that $90 > 92$.
*   **The Setup:** Two triangles with a total area of 90 are cut and rearranged into four shapes that seemingly have a total area greater than 92. 
*   **The Flaw:** The visual representation lies. The lines that appear to form a perfect rectangle actually do not; the lengths are slightly off (e.g., a side labeled "$2+$" looks longer than 2 but is mathematically 1.8). 
*   **Takeaway:** Visual proofs (proofs by picture) or cognitive intuition can easily lock the brain into accepting a false premise. Rigorously writing out step-by-step logical deductions is the only way to expose bugs.

## 3. Ordinary Induction
Induction is the most powerful and commonly used proof technique in computer science. It is fundamentally an axiom.

**The Induction Axiom:**
Let $P(n)$ be a predicate. If $P(0)$ is true, and for all natural numbers $n$, $P(n) \implies P(n+1)$ is true, then for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $P(n)$ is true. 

This is often compared to a chain of dominoes: knocking over the first domino ($P(0)$) guarantees that each subsequent domino will knock over the next ($P(n) \implies P(n+1)$), causing all dominoes to fall.

### Example 1: Sum of the First $n$ Integers
**Theorem:** $\forall n \ge 0, \sum_{i=1}^n i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$.
*   **Warning on Notation:** The notation $1 + 2 + \dots + n$ is ambiguous. For $n=1$, the sum is just 1. For $n \le 0$, the sum evaluates to 0 because there are no terms. The rigorous notation is $\sum_{i=1}^n i$.
*   **Proof:**
    1.  **Induction Hypothesis:** Let $P(n)$ be the proposition that $\sum_{i=1}^n i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$.
    2.  **Base Case:** $n=0$. The sum of zero terms is 0. The formula evaluates to $\frac{0(0+1)}{2} = 0$. Thus, $P(0)$ is true.
    3.  **Inductive Step:** For $n \ge 0$, assume $P(n)$ is true (this is the inductive hypothesis). We must show $P(n+1)$ is true, namely $\sum_{i=1}^{n+1} i = \frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{2}$.
        $$ \sum_{i=1}^{n+1} i = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n i\right) + (n+1) $$
        Substitute using $P(n)$:
        $$ = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} + (n+1) = \frac{n^2 + n + 2n + 2}{2} = \frac{n^2 + 3n + 2}{2} = \frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{2} $$
    4.  **Conclusion:** By induction, the theorem holds for all $n \ge 0$.
    *Note:* Induction proves the statement is true, but it doesn't explain *why* or how to derive the formula initially. 

### Example 2: Divisibility
**Theorem:** For all natural numbers $n$, 3 divides $n^3 - n$.
*   **Proof:**
    1.  **Induction Hypothesis:** $P(n) := 3 | (n^3 - n)$.
    2.  **Base Case:** $P(0)$: $3 | (0^3 - 0) \implies 3 | 0$, which is true.
    3.  **Inductive Step:** Assume $P(n)$. Show $P(n+1)$, which means $3 | ((n+1)^3 - (n+1))$.
        $$ (n+1)^3 - (n+1) = (n^3 + 3n^2 + 3n + 1) - n - 1 = n^3 + 3n^2 + 2n $$
        To use the inductive hypothesis, rearrange to extract $(n^3 - n)$:
        $$ = (n^3 - n) + 3n^2 + 3n $$
        By $P(n)$, $3$ divides $(n^3 - n)$. Clearly, $3$ divides $3n^2$ and $3n$. Therefore, 3 divides the entire expression.

## 4. The "All Horses Are the Same Color" Paradox
To show the pitfalls of induction, we analyze a famous false proof.
**False Theorem:** All horses are the same color.
*   **Bogus Proof:**
    1.  **Hypothesis:** $P(n) =$ in any set of $n$ horses ($n \ge 1$), all horses are the same color.
    2.  **Base Case:** $P(1)$. In any set of 1 horse, the horse is the same color as itself. True.
    3.  **Inductive Step:** Assume $P(n)$. Consider a set of $n+1$ horses, $\{h_1, h_2, \dots, h_n, h_{n+1}\}$.
        *   The first $n$ horses $\{h_1, \dots, h_n\}$ are a set of $n$ horses, so by $P(n)$, they are the same color.
        *   The last $n$ horses $\{h_2, \dots, h_{n+1}\}$ are a set of $n$ horses, so by $P(n)$, they are the same color.
        *   Since $h_1$ matches the color of the middle horses, and $h_{n+1}$ matches the color of the middle horses, $h_1$ must match $h_{n+1}$. Therefore, all $n+1$ horses are the same color. $P(n) \implies P(n+1)$.
*   **The Flaw:** The bug hides in the $\dots$ notation. Consider the transition from $n=1$ to $n=2$ (proving $P(1) \implies P(2)$). If you have a set of 2 horses $\{h_1, h_2\}$, the first $n$ horses is just $\{h_1\}$, and the last $n$ horses is just $\{h_2\}$. The intersection of these two sets is the empty set; there are no "middle horses" to bridge the logical equality between $h_1$ and $h_2$. The inductive step fails for $n=1$, breaking the entire chain. 

## 5. Strengthening the Inductive Hypothesis
Sometimes, proving a specific statement by induction fails because the inductive assumption $P(n)$ is too weak to reach $P(n+1)$. If you make the problem *harder* (strengthen the hypothesis), you give yourself more tools/assumptions for the inductive step.

### Example: Tiling the Courtyard
**Goal:** A courtyard of size $2^n \times 2^n$ needs to be tiled with L-shaped tiles (covering 3 squares each), leaving exactly one specific square empty for a statue of "Bill".
*   **Failed Attempt:** We try to prove $P(n) = $ "A $2^n \times 2^n$ grid with the *center* square missing can be tiled". For the inductive step ($2^{n+1} \times 2^{n+1}$), we divide the grid into four $2^n \times 2^n$ quadrants. However, the missing center of the $2^{n+1} \times 2^{n+1}$ grid only occupies the corner of one sub-quadrant. The other three quadrants have no missing squares, so we cannot apply our $P(n)$ assumption to them.
*   **Strengthening the Hypothesis:** Let $P(n) = $ "A $2^n \times 2^n$ grid with *ANY* square missing can be tiled". 
    1.  **Base Case:** $n=0$. A $1 \times 1$ grid with 1 missing square requires 0 tiles. True.
    2.  **Inductive Step:** Take a $2^{n+1} \times 2^{n+1}$ grid with one missing square anywhere. Divide it into four $2^n \times 2^n$ quadrants. The missing square falls into exactly one quadrant. We place a single L-shaped tile in the center of the entire grid so that it covers exactly one corner square of the other three quadrants. Now, all four $2^n \times 2^n$ quadrants have exactly one square "missing" (either the actual missing square, or covered by the center L-tile). We apply $P(n)$ to tile all four quadrants independently.
    3.  **Conclusion:** By strengthening the hypothesis, the inductive step easily works, and the original specific goal (putting Bill in the center) is proven as a subset of the generalized theorem.
