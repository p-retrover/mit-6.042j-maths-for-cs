# Lecture 1: Proofs, Propositions, and Axioms

## Overview
This lecture establishes the foundational logic required for computer science and mathematics. It contrasts mathematical proofs with everyday methods of ascertaining truth, defines the precise nature of **propositions** and **predicates**, and introduces universal and existential **quantifiers**. It rigorously defines logical connectives such as **implications** and **if and only if** (iff) statements, warning against common intuitive traps. Finally, it outlines the **axiomatic method**, highlighting the importance of consistent and complete axioms while touching upon Gödel's Incompleteness Theorem.

## 1. Ascertaining Truth and Mathematical Proofs
Before discussing mathematics, it is useful to observe how society generally establishes truth. Various fields ascertain truth through different methods:
*   **Observation and Experimentation:** In science and everyday life, truth is often established by observing repeated outcomes, such as a piece of chalk consistently falling to the ground.
*   **Sampling and Counterexamples:** If an experiment is performed ten times with the same result, it might be accepted as truth. Conversely, a single counterexample establishes falsehood.
*   **Authority:** In society, truth is often dictated by a boss (e.g., Donald Trump) or a professor.
*   **Inner Conviction:** Relying on intuition, commonly seen when programmers blindly assert, "There are no bugs in my program".

In mathematics, none of these societal methods are sufficient. Mathematics is unique because authority does not dictate truth; the youngest student can win an argument against the most experienced professor if the mathematics is rigorous. 

**Definition:** A **mathematical proof** is a verification of a proposition by a chain of logical deductions from a set of axioms.

## 2. Propositions
**Definition:** A proposition is a statement that is definitively either true or false. 

Statements like questions ("Who are you?"), greetings ("Hello"), or paradoxes ("This statement is false") are neither true nor false, and thus are *not* propositions.

### The Danger of Sampling: Mathematical Examples
To illustrate why sampling or intuition is insufficient for mathematical proof, consider the following propositions discussed in the lecture:

*   **Example 1:** $2 + 3 = 5$. This is a straightforward true proposition.
*   **Example 2:** "For all $n \in \mathbb{N}$, the polynomial $n^2 + n + 41$ is a prime number". 
    *   If you test the first 40 natural numbers ($n=0, 1, 2 \dots 39$), the predicate yields a prime number every single time (e.g., $n=0 \implies 41$, $n=1 \implies 43$, $n=39 \implies 1601$). 
    *   In fields like physics or statistics, 40 successful trials might establish a rule. However, in mathematics, this proposition is **false**. For $n=40$, $40^2 + 40 + 41 = 1681$, which is $41^2$ and therefore not prime.
*   **Example 3 (Euler's Conjecture):** In 1769, Euler conjectured that $a^4 + b^4 + c^4 = d^4$ has no positive integer solutions. 
    *   After 218 years of failed attempts to find a solution, the proposition was proven **false** by Noam Elkies, who found a valid counterexample ($a=95800, b=217519, c=414560, d=422481$).
*   **Example 4:** $313(x^3 + y^3) = z^3$ has no positive integer solutions. 
    *   This is **false**, but the shortest counterexample has over 1,000 digits. A computer could never exhaustively search enough cases to find this, highlighting why analytical proof is required over brute-force sampling.
*   **Example 5 (Four Color Theorem):** "The regions in any map can be colored in four colors so that adjacent regions have different colors".
    *   Conjectured in 1853, a widely accepted "proof by picture" by Kempe in 1879 was believed for a decade before a fatal flaw was found. Proofs by picture are convincing but notoriously prone to cognitive lock-in and error. The theorem was finally proved true in 1977 using an exhaustive computer check of thousands of cases.
*   **Example 6 (Goldbach's Conjecture):** "Every even integer greater than 2 is the sum of two primes".
    *   This remains an unsolved mystery. A *Boston Globe* article once attempted to explain it using the example "$20 = 9 + 11$", fundamentally misunderstanding the conjecture since 9 is not a prime number. 

## 3. Predicates and Quantifiers
*   **Predicate:** A proposition whose truth depends on the value of one or more variables (e.g., "$n$ is a perfect square").
*   **Universal Quantifier ($\forall$):** Read as "for all." Asserts that a predicate is true for every possible choice of a variable within a specific universe of discourse.
*   **Existential Quantifier ($\exists$):** Read as "there exists." Asserts that there is at least one choice of the variable for which the predicate is true.

## 4. Logical Connectives: Implications and IFF
### Implications ($P \implies Q$)
The statement "$P$ implies $Q$" is defined to be **true** if $P$ is false *or* $Q$ is true.
*   *Mathematical Example:* $\forall n \in \mathbb{Z}, n \ge 2 \implies n^2 \ge 4$. This is True.
*   *Colloquial Example:* "If pigs could fly, I would be king." Because pigs cannot fly ($P$ is false), the implication as a whole evaluates to True, regardless of whether I am king or not.

### If and Only If ($P \iff Q$)
An "if and only if" statement is true exclusively when both $P$ and $Q$ possess the same truth value (both true, or both false). When required to prove an IFF statement, you must rigorously prove both directions: $P \implies Q$ and $Q \implies P$.
*   *Example:* $\forall n \in \mathbb{Z}, n \ge 2 \iff n^2 \ge 4$. This proposition is **false**. If we select $n = -3$, then $(-3)^2 \ge 4$ is true, but $-3 \ge 2$ is false. 

## 5. Axioms and Logical Deductions
### Axioms
**Definition:** Axioms are propositions that are assumed to be true without a proof because they are considered fundamental and reasonable. 

In mathematics, you *must* make assumptions (axioms) to establish a starting point. Any person who agrees with your starting axioms and follows your logical deductions must agree with your conclusion. Axioms can contradict one another if applied in different contexts. A classic example revolves around the parallel line postulate in geometry:
*   **Euclidean (Planar) Geometry:** Given a line $L$ and a point $p$ not on $L$, there is *exactly one* line through $p$ parallel to $L$.
*   **Spherical Geometry:** There are *no* lines through $p$ parallel to $L$.
*   **Hyperbolic Geometry:** There are *infinitely many* lines through $p$ parallel to $L$.

### Guiding Principles of Axioms
A set of axioms should ideally be:
1.  **Consistent:** No proposition can be proved to be both true and false. Inconsistent axioms are catastrophic; if a system is inconsistent, you can prove any falsehood to be true.
2.  **Complete:** Every proposition can be proved either definitively true or definitively false.

*Note:* Mathematician Kurt Gödel demonstrated that it is mathematically impossible for any sufficiently complex set of axioms to be *both* perfectly consistent and complete. Therefore, there are true mathematical facts that will never be able to be proven.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1.1: Spot the Flaw in the Square Root Proof**
What is going on here?!
$1 = \sqrt{1} = \sqrt{(-1)(-1)} = \sqrt{-1}\sqrt{-1} = (\sqrt{-1})^2 = -1$
Identify and explain the mistake(s) in this bogus proof.

**Problem 1.2: The Bogus Inequality**
Identify exactly where the bug is in the following bogus proof:
*Bogus Claim:* $1/8 > 1/4$
*Bogus Proof:*
$3 > 2$
$3 \log_{10}(1/2) > 2 \log_{10}(1/2)$
$\log_{10}(1/2)^3 > \log_{10}(1/2)^2$
$(1/2)^3 > (1/2)^2$
$1/8 > 1/4$.

**Problem 1.3: The Bogus AM-GM Inequality Proof**
There is something objectionable about the following proof that the Arithmetic Mean is at least as large as the Geometric Mean ($\frac{a+b}{2} \ge \sqrt{ab}$ for all $a,b \ge 0$). What is the objection, and how would you fix it?
*Bogus proof:*
$\frac{a+b}{2} \ge \sqrt{ab}$, so
$a+b \ge 2\sqrt{ab}$, so
$a^2 + 2ab + b^2 \ge 4ab$, so
$a^2 - 2ab + b^2 \ge 0$, so
$(a-b)^2 \ge 0$, which we know is true.
